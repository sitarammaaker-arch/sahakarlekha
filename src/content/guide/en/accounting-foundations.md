# Chapter 1 — Foundations of Accounting: a Cooperative Society’s Books

> **After this chapter you will be able to:** explain why bookkeeping matters; apply debit and credit; recognise the five types of accounts; understand the accounting equation and the accounting cycle.

## 1.1 Why is bookkeeping necessary?

Without a written, orderly record, a society’s true financial position cannot be known.

| Risk without records | Benefit of good records |
|---|---|
| No idea where money went | Every rupee accounted for |
| Audit objections, penalties | Clean audit, returns on time |
| Members’ trust shaken | Transparency, trust |
| Wrong dividend/grant figures | Correct profit distribution |

## 1.2 Basic terms

| Term | Plain meaning |
|---|---|
| Transaction | An exchange of money/goods |
| Account | A separate record of one kind of thing |
| Voucher | The entry/proof of a transaction |
| Debit (Dr) / Credit (Cr) | The left / right side of an account |
| Ledger | A separate page for each account |
| Trial Balance | A list of all balances (total Dr = total Cr) |

## 1.3 The double-entry system

**The basic rule:** every transaction has two sides — a debit (Dr) in one account and a credit (Cr) in another, and both amounts are equal.

**Example:** Received ₹5,000 share capital in cash from a member.

| Account | Dr (₹) | Cr (₹) |
|---|---|---|
| Cash | 5,000 | |
| Share Capital | | 5,000 |

> ⚠️ An unbalanced entry is wrong. SahakarLekha will not save such an entry (Dr = Cr is mandatory).

## 1.4 Golden rules of debit & credit

| Nature | On increase | On decrease |
|---|---|---|
| Asset | Dr | Cr |
| Expense | Dr | Cr |
| Liability | Cr | Dr |
| Capital | Cr | Dr |
| Income | Cr | Dr |

## 1.5 The five types of accounts

| Type | Example in a society |
|---|---|
| Asset | Cash, bank, stock, godown, recoverables |
| Liability | Payable to suppliers, bank loan, deposits |
| Capital/Fund | Share capital, reserve fund, surplus |
| Income | Sales, interest income, admission fee |
| Expense | Purchases, salary, rent, depreciation |

## 1.6 The accounting equation

**Assets = Liabilities + Capital.** Cooperative form: Assets = external liabilities + member funds (share capital + reserves + surplus). After every correct entry it stays balanced — this is the basis of the Balance Sheet tallying.

## 1.7 The accounting cycle

Transaction → voucher → ledger (Dr = Cr) → trial balance → final accounts (Trading → Income & Expenditure → Balance Sheet) → audit and returns. In SahakarLekha you only make the entries; the rest is built automatically — but the understanding must be yours.

## ⚠️ Common mistakes

| Mistake | Avoid by |
|---|---|
| Reversing Dr/Cr | Check the rules table |
| Recording only one side | Remember double-entry |
| Treating an expense as an asset | "Spend = Dr, earn = Cr" |

## 🔍 Audit note
"In the society’s entries the total of debits equals the total of credits; adherence to the double-entry principle is satisfactory."

## 📘 Cooperative standard
Cooperative societies keep their books on the **double-entry** system and the **accrual** basis.

## ✅ Worked example
Four transactions in a day: (1) ₹5,000 share capital in cash; (2) ₹10,000 fertiliser on credit; (3) ₹2,000 cash sale to a member; (4) ₹8,000 salary in cash.

| Account | Dr (₹) | Cr (₹) |
|---|---|---|
| Cash | 5,000 | |
| Share Capital | | 5,000 |
| Fertiliser Purchase | 10,000 | |
| IFFCO (payable) | | 10,000 |
| Cash | 2,000 | |
| Sales | | 2,000 |
| Salary | 8,000 | |
| Cash | | 8,000 |

## 📚 Case study
A clerk kept only a cash register, not ledgers. At year-end there was no answer to "how much stock, who owes us?" **Lesson:** every account needs its own ledger.

## ❓ FAQ
**Q. The app does it automatically, so why understand Dr/Cr?** So you can catch errors and read the reports.
**Q. Is share capital income or capital?** Capital — it is the member’s contribution, not earnings.
